THE BIRTH CONTROL PILL

The birth control pill

The classic birth control pill is a widely used and reliable method of contraception. It consists of an estrogen and a progestin (sex hormones). Different progestins can produce different side effects, which are taken into account when choosing a preparation. In addition to many positive effects such as reduced menstrual cramps, cycle control, or improvement of acne, side effects can occur. The most medically feared side effect is thrombosis (blood clot formation in veins). To assess risk, we recommend an APC test to rule out an often undiagnosed hereditary clotting disorder. A special case is the so‑called "mini‑pill." This is an estrogen‑free pill taken continuously without a break. Usually, menstrual bleeding stops while taking the mini‑pill.

The hormonal IUD

The hormonal IUD is a local contraceptive device that is placed in the uterine cavity. It releases the progestin levonorgestrel in minimal doses. The IUD is available in three versions: “Jaydess” for 3 years, and “Kyleena” and “Mirena” for 5 years each. With Jaydess, light menstrual bleeding usually continues. Kyleena is the same size as the smaller Jaydess but, like Mirena, has a hormone reservoir for 5 years. Menstrual bleeding occurs less often compared to the 3‑year hormonal IUD. With Mirena, menstrual bleeding usually stops after some time. This can be very helpful if you suffer from painful periods or heavy bleeding related to hormonal changes. The hormonal IUD is one of the most reliable contraception methods and has very few side effects, since the hormones act mainly in the uterus and only a very small amount enters the rest of the body.

Implanon® (contraceptive implant)

Implanon is a soft plastic rod containing the hormone etonogestrel. It is inserted under the skin of the inner upper arm by a gynecologist. It continuously releases hormones and provides contraceptive protection for up to 3 years. Although its hormone composition is generally better tolerated than the birth control pill, it can still cause irregular bleeding or absence of menstruation.

The 3-month injection

The 3-month injection is also a hormonal contraceptive method that works by delivering the progestin hormone. The injection must be given by a gynecologist every three months. The 3-month injection puts the ovaries and the uterine lining into a kind of resting state. After initial irregularities in menstrual bleeding, bleeding usually stops completely. Like the hormonal IUD, the 3-month injection is also often used to treat heavy bleeding due to hormonal changes. In most cases, menstrual bleeding stops, without bringing on menopause prematurely.

Hormone-free

Not every woman tolerates hormonal contraception. For some women, hormones are medically contraindicated due to inherited clotting disorders. Common unwanted effects of hormonal contraception include weight gain, fluid retention, or mood changes. Many women who have taken hormones for years only realize how much they were affected after stopping the pill. For those women, a hormone-free alternative can provide a significant gain in quality of life. They keep their natural cycle and are not affected by the side effects of hormonal contraception.

The copper IUD

A very effective method of contraception and the longest-established intrauterine device. IUDs are now available in many sizes and shapes and are therefore well tolerated. The copper IUD provides reliable contraceptive protection for 3 years.

The gold IUD

The gold IUD is very similar in use to the copper IUD and provides local contraception within the uterus. The gold ions released also have spermicide effects. Compared to the copper IUD and the copper chain, the gold IUD has the advantage that it can remain in the uterus for up to 5 years when well tolerated. This provides reliable contraceptive protection over a long period.
